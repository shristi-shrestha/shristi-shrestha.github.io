## An Unconventional Start

My journey into bioinformatics didn't begin in a traditional research laboratory. In fact, it started in one of the least conventional places for a PhD student, a sequencing core facility.

While many PhD students spend their years surrounded by fellow graduate students, postdocs, and principal investigators focused on a single biological question, my environment looked very different. I was the only PhD student in a team of highly experienced experimental scientists whose mission wasn't to publish papers or pursue fundamental research. Instead, they delivered high-quality sequencing data to a diverse community of life science researchers.

Every day, samples arrived from different laboratories with different questions. Our job was to transform tissues into reliable sequencing-ready data. Success depended on consistency, precision, and efficiency.

Looking back, I couldn't have asked for better mentors.

Most of my colleagues had over a decade of experience in molecular biology and had witnessed the evolution of sequencing technologies firsthand—from older sequencing platforms to modern high-throughput instruments. They had refined nucleic acid extraction protocols for virtually every tissue imaginable, adapted to ever-improving library preparation methods, and understood the importance of rigorous quality control long before sequencing began.

They also possessed a kind of practical knowledge that rarely appears in textbooks. They knew which extraction kits consistently worked, which vendors provided the most reliable reagents and instruments, and where small protocol adjustments could make the difference between a failed run and high-quality sequencing data.

As a young scientist, I absorbed not only laboratory techniques but also a philosophy of scientific work: robust methods matter, reproducibility matters, and quality begins long before data reaches a computer.

That experience fundamentally shaped how I approach bioinformatics today. Whenever I analyze sequencing data in R, I still think about the biological material it came from, the extraction methods that were used, the quality control metrics collected before sequencing, and the countless decisions made at the bench that influence every downstream result.

Although my work has shifted from pipettes to code, I've never really left the laboratory behind. I simply carried its lessons into a different stage of the sequencing workflow.
